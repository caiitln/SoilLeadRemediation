Using Plants to Reduce the Dangers of Contaminated Residential Soil

A review of why lead in soil is dangerous and how one plant is the innovative solution outcompeting current remediation methods

Photo by Austin Ban via Unsplash

Introduction

Lead is one of the most harmful toxins in the environment. Its presence persists in soil despite decades of regulation on the metal. Soil is a major source of lead exposure for people today. This comes from soil trailing into homes as house dust coupled with kids’ tendency to play in and ingest soil (Laidlaw et al., 2017). Remediating lead-contaminated soil is urgently needed, since exposure to low concentrations of lead could result in adverse health effects due to bioaccumulation.

Previous data show that after soil remediation in yards, the amount of lead in the blood of surrounding residents, especially children, is substantially reduced. The hindrance of removing lead from soil is that this process is typically expensive and labor-intensive, giving it the reputation as being impractical. It is even less practical for low-income families residing in urban areas, even though their lead exposure risk is higher (Kraft and Scheberle, 1995).

Developed through research funded by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development, a new technology called chelate-assisted phytoremediation presents a possible solution to this problem. It involves planting vetiver grass in one’s yard. This grass has a massive root system that can absorb lead from the soil and prevent as much soil from becoming house dust. A biodegradable substance, ethylenediaminedisuccinic acid (EDDS), is sprayed on the soil to facilitate plant absorption of the lead. In this paper, the need for soil lead remediation, traditional remediation methods, and most importantly the phytoremediation approach will be presented as an introduction to and reference for soil lead remediation in residential areas. 

"The most important thing about using plants is that it is an environment-friendly method, but also easy for the homeowners to understand and implement. No special expertise or equipment would be needed." —Dr. Rupali Datta, Michigan Technological University

The Need for Soil Lead Remediation

Photo by Halacious via Unsplash

Old exterior paint chipping off the side of a building

While lead is well known for having caused contamination through mining, smelters, batteries, paint, and gasoline (Kerr and Newell, 2003; Laidlaw et al., 2017; Kushwaha et al., 2018), less attention tends to be paid to soil as a source of contamination (Mielke et al., 2007). Soil has become ridden with lead after being widely used for much of history (Laidlaw et al., 2017). Despite popular uses of lead being banned decades ago, the properties of lead allow it to survive in soil for centuries (Kerr and Newell, 2003; Schwarz et al., 2012). In residential areas, lead-based paint in house dust and soils has been identified as a persistent source of lead poisoning. Urban soil has been referred to as a sink for lead (Mielke et al., 2007; Schwarz et al., 2012).

Like with soil, lead is not eliminated from the body quickly. Even exposure to lead in low quantities builds up over time (Gürer et al., 2001). There is almost no function in the body that is not negatively affected by lead. High levels of lead significantly disrupt the functions of the digestive system, nervous system, respiratory system, reproductive system, enzymes, DNA transcription, and bones, etc. (Ab Latif Wani et al., 2015).

There is almost no function in the body that is not negatively affected by lead.

Recently, more interest lies within the effects of low levels of lead. As more studies revealed majorly harmful effects of low levels of lead, the Centers for Disease Control (CDC) continuously decreased the amount they considered the standard limit for blood lead levels (BLLs). Since 2012, the CDC’s limit is to be below 5 μg/dL for children and below 10 μg/dL for adults (CDC, 2021; Ab Latif Wani et al., 2015). 5 μg/dL means that for every deciliter of blood, there are 5 micrograms of lead present. To put that into perspective, 5 micrograms is just a fraction of what a single eyelash would weigh, while a deciliter is a bit less than half a cup of liquid. That said, no amount of lead in the body, not even such low ones, is considered safe (USDH-NTP, 2012).

In 2012, the United States Department of Health’s National Toxicology Program produced a review of the impacts of BLLs below the CDC limits (USDH-NTP, 2012). The Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry published a similar review in 2020 (ATSDR, 2020). Each of these gather data from international studies on the effects of low BLLs on humans in order to highlight the most reliable and consistent results. Below is a consolidated list of some of the effects they wrote on:

Promotional image from the 2020 National Lead Poisoning Prevention Week from the CDC

Photo by Jelleke Vanooteghem via Unsplash

In children:

  • Lower academic achievement and intelligence quotients (IQs) in children with BLLs less than 5 μg/dL
  • More attention-related behavioral problems in children with BLLs less than 5 μg/dL
  • Delayed puberty, decreased hearing, and increased hypersensitivity (allergies) for children with BLLs less than 10 μg/dL

In adults:

  • Increased incidence of essential tremor and risk of hypertension, heart disease, mortality due to cardiovascular disease, asthma, obstructive lung disease, and all cancers for adults with BLLs less than 5 μg/dL
  • Decreased function for attention, memory, and learning for adults with BLLs less than 5 μg/dL
  • Decreased fertility for both sexes and increased risk of spontaneous abortions, preterm birth, and earlier onset of menopause in females with BLLs less than 5 μg/dL

Children and adults with BLLs above the listed ranges are at greater risk for these issues. These lists are not exhaustive of all the effects of low levels of lead. The entire compilation of effects can be read about in the aforementioned reviews (USDH-NTP, 2012; ATSDR, 2020).

Figure 1: Diagram displaying how children and adults are exposed to lead-contaminated soil.

Lead in residential soil is ingested by children and inhaled by both children and adults (Figure 1). Adults and children inhale lead when the soil lead enters the home and becomes house dust (Lanphear and Roghmann, 1997; Bornschein et al., 1991). Soil lead can be tracked into the house on shoes or even by pets (Hunt and Johnson, 2012; Brunekreef et al., 1987). Children pick up and ingest soil more frequently due to their tendency to crawl on the ground, play with soil, and touch their mouths (Viverette et al., 1996). This, combined with the fact that children’s bodies are rapidly developing, puts them at a significantly higher risk to have elevated BLLs (USDH-NTP, 2012).

"Lead in soil has been an overlooked problem. People typically think of kids getting exposed through paint, but studies really show it is dust, which is traced right to the soil." —Dr. Dibyendu Sarkar, Stevens Institute of Technology

BLLs have repeatedly been found to increase alongside increased soil lead levels (Mielke et al., 2007; Lindern et al., 2003; Aschengrau et al., 1994; Weitzman et al., 1993). Studies have shown that when lead in soil is remediated, the lead levels of house dust and the BLLs of residents decrease (Mielke et al., 2007; Lindern et al., 2003; Aschengrau et al., 1994; Weitzman et al., 1993).

When lead in soil is remediated, the lead levels of house dust and the BLLs of residents decrease.

For example, a Boston study was conducted with three groups of children (see Figure 2). Group A’s homes had the soil, interior dust, and any loose paint treated. Group B received just interior dust and loose paint treatment. Group C received only loose paint treatment. After factoring in the initial BLLs of the groups, the children from Group A had an average BLL 1.28 μg/dL lower than Group B and 1.49 μg/dL lower than Group C just 11 months later (Weitzman et al., 1993). This demonstrates just how powerful soil remediation can be.

Figure 2: A study in Boston treated three groups of homes for lead contamination. The group who received soil treatment saw the greatest reductions in BLLs.

While there are a few medical treatments that aim to reverse lead toxicity, preventing exposure to lead in the first place is by far the safer and more sustainable solution (Ab Latif Wani et al., 2015). It is clear that remediating lead-contaminated soil is a necessary part of preventing exposure due to the connection between BLLs and soil lead levels discussed here. Now, the task is to figure out how to remediate lead-contaminated soil. 

Traditional Remediation Methods

There are numerous methods for remediating lead-contaminated soil. The methods can be loosely grouped into two major categories: chemical and physical. Chemical methods involve changing what is actually in the soil matter, whereas physical methods focus on replacing the soil altogether or creating barriers that make it less accessible.

Physical Methods

Photo by Abdul Zreika via Unsplash

Excavation of soil

A seemingly obvious way to remediate lead-contaminated soil would be to replace the soil in a yard with clean soil, however this is impractical for a variety of reasons. This method is known as excavation, or off-site disposal. Disposing of contaminated soil elsewhere is not only expensive and labor-intensive; it requires having a source to legally dump massive amounts of contaminated soil onto and a source of clean soil to take from (Hettiarachchi and Pierzynski, 2004; Diamond et al., 2009; Laidlaw et al., 2017). It is unrealistic for homeowners to accomplish, negatively disrupts the local environment, and does nothing to actually reduce the volume or toxicity of lead present in the environment as a whole (Royer et al., 1992; Hettiarachchi and Pierzynski, 2004).

Some barrier-based physical methods are considered the most realistic for homeowners. What makes these physical methods different and not actually ideal is that they do not set out to ‘fix’ the soil, they only change how people use their yards. This involves placing stone pathways on frequently walked areas, relocating play areas to less contaminated parts of the yard, or installing raised beds of clean soil for gardening, playing, or eating outside (Hynes et al., 2001; Litt et al., 2002).

While these methods may be favorable in contrast to other pricey options, they do not offer true solutions and are not actually what many homeowners would consider ‘cheap’. One study using this method found that the average cost per yard was about $2,100, not including the price of materials donated by local companies for the sake of the project or any overhead costs (Hynes et al., 2001).

Additionally, it is unclear whether raised beds of clean soil are even able to resist becoming contaminated overtime; one study has shown that they become recontaminated (Clark et al., 2008). Replacing the top few inches of soil with clean soil in entire yards has also been used as an ‘affordable’ intervention. This resulted in recontamination after the hefty price of $9,600 per yard (Aschengrau et al., 1994).

Play area placed in yard that avoids direct contact with soil, acting as a barrier

Chemical Methods

A chemical option with many cons similar to that of the excavation method is soil washing. This consists of first transporting the soil to a facility, and then putting it through a series of actions that clean it of its contaminated parts so that the same soil can be filled into the yard again (Diamond et al., 2009; Laidlaw et al., 2017).

In theory, remediating soil without having to move it from the yard can make for more feasible methods for homeowners. That said, the options for treating soil in place tend to be inaccessible to the general public. For instance, lead in soil can be solidified or stabilized, making it less bioavailable, or accessible for the human body to take in. This often requires injecting proprietary additives into the soil or using complicated, inaccessible equipment (Royer et al., 1992).

Using chemicals, specifically phosphorus, to stabilize the lead in soil is overall one of the least expensive options (Basta and McGowen, 2004). A study verifying a positive impact on residents has not yet been done (Laidlaw et al., 2017). This method has some dependence on being able to control certain environmental factors; real-life settings such as residential yards can thus make this method more difficult to implement (Scheckel et al., 2013).

Phytoremediation as an Accessible Solution

"Dig and haul is extremely expensive. Two major components make phytoremediation attractive: it is visually unobtrusive and really inexpensive. The only cheaper alternative is to let the lead stay there, which we cannot do." —Dr. Dibyendu Sarkar, Stevens Institute of Technology

All hope is not lost, though. There is another strategy for remediating soil that has been proving itself to hold up better in terms of cost, accessibility, ease of use, and sustainability: phytoremediation. Phytoremediation uses plants as a solution to contaminated soil. Phytoremediation for lead contaminated soil involves two techniques: phytoextraction and phytostabilization.

Figure 3: Phytoextraction vs. Phytostabilization

Phytostabilization is when a plant makes the lead less mobile or available for humans to uptake it. This can translate to a reduction in house dust lead levels, since less soil is being brought into the home as dust. For phytoextraction, on the other hand, plants remove lead from soil by acting like sponges (Figure 3). The roots of the plant soak up lead surrounding it like a sponge would with water. Eventually, lead is transported up to the leaves and is stored in the roots and leaves of the plant, which can be harvested and disposed of. Phytoextraction outcompetes phytostabilization in removing lead from soil rather than fixing it in the soil (Figure 3).

Of course, not just any plant lends itself to phytoremediation. The plant must be able to tolerate high levels of lead, as well as survive in a variety of soil conditions and climates. Other ideal characteristics include being a plant that grows quickly, is high in mass, has an extensive root system, and is easy to harvest (Datta and Sarkar, 2004).

Vetiver grass being planted into a residential yard

Based on those measures, vetiver grass is an ideal candidate for phytoremediation. Vetiver grows quickly and reaches heights of about 1-2 yards, while its root system is massive and complex, growing to be about 3-4 yards long (Truong 2000). This large root system makes vetiver ideal for preventing soil erosion since it holds the soil particles together (Danh et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2000).

Vetiver is highly tolerant of extreme climatic conditions, including drought, flood, and temperatures from -4°F to 131°F (Danh et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2000). Most importantly, it is also highly tolerant of metals in soil, including lead, and can adapt to a variety of soil conditions (Danh et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2000).

Although vetiver has been proven to accumulate around 19,800 mg/kg of lead in water (Andra et al., 2009), it can only uptake (or remove) the amount of lead that is available to it. When lead is available for plant uptake, it is considered soluble. The portion of soluble lead in soil is considerably low (Andra et al., 2010). Thus, additives known as chelating agents are used to increase lead solubility (Andra et al., 2009; Attiniti et al., 2017). In other words, a substance is added to the soil that makes it easier for the plant to uptake more lead.

Freshly watered vetiver grass in a yard

It is important that the added substance does not compromise one of the major pros of phytoremediation: being safe and sustainable for the soil and environment. Some chelating agents are more effective at increasing plant uptake than others but are not as sustainable (Attiniti et al., 2017). Thus, they are not good candidates for phytoremediation. Among all chelating agents, ethylenediaminedisuccinic acid (EDDS) is an effective one due to being effective and environmentally-friendly (Attiniti et al., 2017; Sarkar et al., 2008). It is biodegradable, which decays in soil naturally in a short time.

The research team of Prof. Dibyendu Sarkar at Stevens Institute of Technology has proved the effectiveness of EDDS in helping vetiver uptake more lead, making EDDS a great option for assisting lead phytoextraction. Phase I of this research began in 2004, funded by the United States Department of Housing and Urban Development’s (HUD’s) Lead Technical Studies Grant Program (HUD LTS – TXLHT0102-04). EDDS was tested in phytoremediation in a controlled greenhouse setting. Once this was successful, HUD provided more funding in 2010 for Phase II (NJLHT0107-10). The team conducted field experiments using soil boxes in natural conditions.

It was not until after Phase II was proven successful that the team was able to begin testing in real resident’s yards in Jersey City and San Antonio. This is Phase III of the project (funded by HUD with MILTS0007-17) and has been underway since 2017, set to be finished this year. Within just two years, most of the yards have been “cleaned” with their lead soil concentrations all the way down to being below 400mg/Kg.

Figure 4: Major steps in the phytoremediation process for residents

To complete phytoremediation in an actual yard (Figure 4), the first step would be determining where the treatment would be most helpful. Typically, this would be in the contaminated parts of the yard with lead concentrations higher than the USEPA soil Pb hazard cutoff value of 400 mg/kg for soil in children’s play areas. Although it is visually unobtrusive, vetiver grass could grow too tall to easily walk through. If a homeowner wanted to remediate all of the soil in a yard, it would be best to do so in sections (completing one before starting another), since the vetiver will be completely removed after treatment is complete.

The vetiver, which is grown off-site, can be planted into the chosen area of the yard. The chelating agent, EDDS, can then be applied in the same method one would apply a spray-on fertilizer. EDDS is not a hazardous substance to handle. If there is no rain, the homeowner only needs to water the vetiver grass to keep it growing.

Thus, the residents are already better off just from planting the vetiver before any lead is taken up.

During the phytoremediation process, the vetiver grass roots can hold soil in place and prevent it from becoming house dust. Thus, the residents are already better off just from planting the vetiver before any lead is taken up. Still, the bigger and safer goal is to have the lead ultimately removed from the soil. Soil samples may be tested to track the removal process of lead in the soil. Once the lead levels have reduced to safe levels, the vetiver may be removed and the treatment is complete. The length of time it takes to reach this point varies greatly depending on soil conditions, the climate, and other uncontrollable factors. Quantifications of lead concentrations in soil and application of EDDS could be consulted by Prof. Dibyendu Sarkar’s research team at Stevens Institute of Technology.

Overall, the planting and maintenance of the grass is straightforward and requires only a few basic gardening tools. It is clear that phytoremediation is more feasible than any other soil treatment method, especially for homeowners.

Conclusion

Photo by Scott Webb via Unsplash

Having access to practical forms of soil remediation is a necessity. Living in a home and having outdoor space free of lead contamination should not be a luxury, yet today it is treated like one. It is all too common for families to face the burden of constant lead exposure.

With the high costs and labor associated with traditional remediation methods, it is easy to feel like there are no good options. Accepting that you or your child may face seriously dangerous health consequences, from learning abilities to cancer, is not the only choice. The risk is too high yet has unfortunately been forced onto homeowners for years. Phytoremediation provides a way for homeowners to regain control over their health and their living environment quickly and safely.

"From my interactions with homeowners, I know that most homeowners are not even aware of the extent of lead contamination in their yards, and their children could be inadvertently exposed to lead while playing in their yards. Once we do our survey and let the homeowners see the lead levels in their yards, most often they are shocked and worried. I believe being aware of the issue, and having the tools to deal with it would be empowering to the homeowners." —Dr. Rupali Datta, Michigan Technological University
